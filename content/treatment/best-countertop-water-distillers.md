Most people who land on a distiller after researching water treatment are not trying to filter a whole house. They want very clean water for one specific job: a CPAP reservoir that the manufacturer says needs distilled, a steam iron that keeps scaling up, an aquarium, or drinking water in a rental where they cannot install anything under the sink. A countertop distiller does that one job well. Whether it is the right tool for your everyday drinking water is a separate question, and the honest answer is usually no.

This is an informational buying guide, not a star-rated test. We have not run a head-to-head lab test of distiller brands, so the framing here is how the technology works, what it costs to live with, the certification to look for, and the situations where we would consider one over reverse osmosis.

## How a Countertop Distiller Works

A countertop distiller is a small appliance, usually about the size of a large coffee maker, that boils water and then catches the steam.

You pour tap water into a stainless steel boiling chamber, press start, and a heating element brings it to a boil. As the water boils, steam rises and passes through a condenser, often a coil cooled by a small fan. The steam turns back into liquid and drips into a collection container, usually a glass carafe or a plastic jug. Most dissolved minerals, heavy metals, nitrates, and many bacteria and viruses have boiling points well above water, so they stay behind in the boiling chamber as a scaly residue you clean out later.

The result is water with near-zero total dissolved solids. A [TDS meter](/testing/what-is-tds-in-water/) will typically read distilled output at 0 to 2 mg/L, lower than reverse osmosis, which usually lands at 10 to 40 mg/L.

Most units add a small activated carbon sachet or post-filter at the spout. That step matters more than it looks, and we will come back to it.

## The VOC Problem Distillation Has

Distillation is excellent at removing anything that will not evaporate. It is weaker on things that do.

Volatile organic compounds, the category that includes chloroform and other disinfection byproducts, benzene, and solvents, have boiling points close to or below water. During the boil, some of them can volatilize and carry over into the steam, ending up in your "distilled" water. A bare distiller with no carbon stage can pass VOCs through and, in some cases, concentrate them slightly relative to the original.

This is why the carbon sachet at the spout is not optional in practice. The carbon catches volatile compounds that survived the boil. If you are considering a distiller specifically because you have a VOC concern, that is exactly the case where reverse osmosis paired with carbon pre-filtration tends to do better. The full trade-off is laid out in the [reverse osmosis versus distillation comparison](/treatment/reverse-osmosis-vs-distillation/).

## What to Look for: NSF/ANSI 62

The certification that applies to home distillers is [NSF/ANSI 62](/learn/nsf-certification-standards-explained/), the standard for drinking water distillation systems. It covers materials, design, and contaminant reduction performance for point-of-use and point-of-entry distillers.

A unit certified to NSF/ANSI 62 has had specific reduction claims verified by an accredited third party rather than asserted by the manufacturer. The standard recognizes that a given system may be effective against some contaminants and not others, so a certification is tied to the particular contaminants the unit was tested for, not a blanket promise.

Here is the honest state of this category: independent NSF/ANSI 62 certification on consumer countertop distillers is not common. Many popular units carry general marketing claims and component listings rather than a full NSF/ANSI 62 contaminant certification. That does not automatically mean a unit performs poorly, but it does mean you are relying on the physics of distillation and the manufacturer's word rather than verified reduction data. Where a unit is certified, that is worth more than a longer claimed contaminant list with no certification behind it. Treat any specific percentage you see without a citation as marketing until you find the documentation.

## What Distilled Water Does Not Give You: Minerals

Distillation strips calcium and magnesium along with everything else, so distilled water is essentially mineral-free. That is the point for a humidifier or a steam iron, where mineral content is what causes the white dust and the scale. It is also why distilled water tastes flat to a lot of people.

This is the same mineral question that comes up with reverse osmosis, only more so, because distilled output is even lower in TDS. We do not make a health claim for distilled water in either direction here. For most people on a varied diet, the minerals in drinking water are a small share of total intake compared to food. The fuller discussion, including what the WHO actually said about low-mineral water and why we treat the "RO water drains your body" claim with caution, lives on the page covering whether [reverse osmosis removes minerals](/treatment/does-reverse-osmosis-remove-minerals/). Everything there applies to distilled water at least as strongly.

If flat taste is the only thing bothering you, the fix is the same as for RO: a pinch of mineral drops, or simply getting used to it.

## The Two Things That Make or Break Daily Use

Two practical realities decide whether a distiller fits your routine.

**Speed.** A typical 4-liter countertop unit makes about 1 gallon in 4 to 6 hours. For one person filling a carafe overnight, that is fine. For a family running drinking water, coffee, and cooking, that math gets tight fast, and you end up planning around the machine.

**Energy.** A distiller draws roughly 600 to 1,000 watts for the length of the cycle and uses about 0.7 to 1.0 kWh per gallon. At roughly the US average residential rate near 18 cents per kWh, that lands somewhere around 10 to 20 cents per gallon, plus replacement carbon sachets and periodic descaling of the boiling chamber. Reverse osmosis, by contrast, runs on your home's water pressure and has essentially no energy cost beyond filter changes, which is part of why an under-sink RO is usually cheaper to live with for high-volume drinking water. If TDS reduction is your goal, the [how to reduce TDS guide](/treatment/how-to-reduce-tds-drinking-water/) walks through where each method fits.

Neither of these is a dealbreaker for the right use. They are dealbreakers for the wrong one.

## Batch vs. Automatic Units

There are two broad formats.

Single-batch countertop distillers are the common ones. You fill the chamber, run a cycle, and the unit stops when the batch is done. They are cheaper, simpler, and easy to move. The limit is capacity, you get one batch at a time and then have to clean and refill.

Automatic or plumbed-in distillers refill themselves and can run continuous cycles into a larger reserve tank. They cost more and take up more space, and some connect to a water line. These make sense for steady higher-volume needs, but at that point the convenience and operating cost of an under-sink RO are usually competitive.

For most home buyers looking at a distiller, the single-batch countertop unit is the realistic option.

## A Note on Buying

Because we have not lab-tested specific models, we are not going to hand you a ranked list of brands with invented scores. What we would actually weigh before buying:

- Whether the unit carries an NSF/ANSI 62 certification for the contaminants you care about, or only general claims.
- Whether it includes a carbon post-filter for the VOC carryover issue, and how much replacement sachets cost over a year.
- A stainless steel boiling chamber and a glass collection carafe rather than plastic, if container material matters to you for taste.
- How easy the boiling chamber is to descale, since that scale is unavoidable and you will be cleaning it regularly.
- Realistic capacity for your household given the 4-to-6-hour-per-gallon ceiling.

## How a Distiller Compares to the Alternatives

| Method | Output TDS | Speed | Operating cost | VOC handling | Installation |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Countertop distiller | 0 to 2 mg/L | 1 gal / 4-6 hrs | Electricity, roughly 10-20 cents/gal | Weak without carbon post-filter | None, plug in |
| Under-sink RO | 10 to 40 mg/L | On demand at the tap | Filter changes only | Strong with carbon pre-filter | Under-sink plumbing |
| Countertop RO | 10 to 40 mg/L | Minutes per liter | Filter changes, small pump | Strong with carbon stage | None, plug in |

The pattern is consistent. Distillation produces the lowest-mineral water of the three and needs no plumbing, but it is the slowest and the only one with an ongoing energy cost. For the humidifier and CPAP side of this decision specifically, the [distilled vs. filtered water for humidifiers guide](/learn/humidifier-distilled-vs-filtered-water/) covers why distilled is the common recommendation there.

## When We Would Consider a Distiller

A distiller earns its place in a few situations.

**Best for specialty near-zero-TDS needs.** CPAP reservoirs, steam irons, certain aquarium setups, and home lab or photography use all want water with as little dissolved content as possible, and distillation delivers that more completely than RO.

**Best for a renter or small kitchen with one person to serve.** No plumbing, no drilling, and the slow batch cycle is manageable when you are only making water for yourself. A [countertop RO system](/treatment/reverse-osmosis/best-countertop-ro-systems/) is the faster no-plumbing alternative if speed matters more than reaching the lowest possible TDS.

**Best for off-grid or backup use where you have power but not water pressure.** A distiller can run off a generator, which RO cannot do without a pump and pressure.

Where we would not reach for one first is everyday drinking water in a busy household. The slow output and the energy cost stack up, and an under-sink or countertop RO covers the same contaminant ground faster and cheaper to run.

---

## Test First, Then Decide

The distiller-versus-everything-else question is downstream of a more basic one: what is actually in your water, and how much of it do you need to remove. Source water varies by region and by well, so test your water before choosing treatment. If your tap is already low in the contaminants you are worried about, you may not need a method that strips the water down to near-zero TDS at all. If you have a genuine specialty need for very clean water, or you simply want the no-plumbing simplicity for one person, a countertop distiller is a reasonable, if slow, way to get there.
