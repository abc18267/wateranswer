A water softener is a bigger plumbing job than a faucet filter and a smaller one than people fear. You are cutting into the cold main, dropping in a bypass valve, running two drain lines, and telling the controller how hard your water is. If you can make a watertight joint, you can do this in an afternoon.

The job has four parts that matter: where you tap the main, the bypass loop, the drain line air gap, and the first regeneration. Get those right and the rest is following the diagram in the box.

A note before you buy anything: test your water first. Source water varies by region and by well, and the softener controller needs your real hardness number in grains per gallon, plus your iron level, to size the salt dose correctly. If you have not run a hardness test yet, do that before you size or program the unit. For how softeners actually trade hardness ions for sodium, see [how a water softener works](/your-water/contaminants/hard-water/how-water-softener-works/).

## What Comes in the Box

A typical whole-house ion exchange softener ships as two tanks plus the plumbing parts. Before you start, confirm your kit has:

- Resin tank with the control head (valve) already attached or ready to thread on
- Brine tank, usually with a brine well tube and float assembly inside
- Bypass valve that snaps or threads onto the control head
- Inlet and outlet adapters to match your pipe size, often 3/4 inch and 1 inch options
- Flexible drain line for the control head
- Brine tank overflow elbow and a separate overflow line
- Hardware clips and O-rings

The two tanks are heavy once they are full of resin and salt, so set them in their final spot before you connect anything. Keep the manufacturer diagram out the whole time. Brands like SpringWell, Aquasana, and Whirlpool/EcoWater all use the same basic layout, but the exact bypass and adapter design differs by model.

## Decide Where to Tap the Main First

This decision shapes the whole install, so make it before you cut anything.

The softener goes on the cold main, downstream of the water meter and any well pressure tank, and upstream of the water heater. That order matters. Tapping after the meter means the whole house gets soft water. Tapping before the heater means your hot water is softened too, which protects the heater from scale.

Keep your outdoor hose bibs and irrigation on the hard side, ahead of the softener. Soft water on a lawn wastes salt, and the added sodium does not help plants or soil. Most homes already branch the outside spigots off before the main reaches the utility area, so this often takes care of itself. If yours does not, plan a hard-water branch ahead of the softener.

You also need a drain within reach. The control head produces backwash water during regeneration, and that water has to go somewhere with an air gap. A floor drain, a standpipe, or a utility sink within a few feet of the install location is what you want. No nearby drain is the single most common reason a DIY softener install turns into a plumber call.

## Tools You'll Need

- Pipe cutter sized for your main
- Fittings to match your pipe: push-fit, compression, soldered copper, or PEX crimp
- Two adjustable wrenches
- Teflon tape for threaded joints
- Torch, flux, and solder (only if soldering copper)
- Level
- Bucket and towels

## Step-by-Step Installation

**1. Shut off the main and drain the system.**
Close the main shutoff valve. Open the lowest faucet in the house and a tub spout on an upper floor so the lines drain and the pressure bleeds off. Cut power to an electric water heater or set a gas heater to pilot, so it can never fire while the lines are empty.

**2. Set the tanks and dry-fit the bypass.**
Place the resin tank and brine tank in their final spot, close to the main and the drain. Snap or thread the bypass valve onto the control head. Note which port is marked IN and which is marked OUT. Flow direction is not optional. Water has to enter the IN side, pass down through the resin, and leave the OUT side.

**3. Plumb the inlet and outlet.**
Cut the cold main where the softener will tie in. Run a line from the house main into the bypass IN port and a line from the bypass OUT port back to the house plumbing that feeds the water heater and the cold fixtures. Use unions or push-fit connectors at the softener so you can disconnect the unit later for service without cutting pipe again. Keep the connections to the bypass hand-tight plus a snug wrench turn. Overtightening the plastic bypass body cracks it.

**4. Run the drain line.**
Connect the flexible drain line to the drain port on the control head. Route it to your floor drain, standpipe, or utility sink. The end of the drain line must sit above the drain with a physical air gap, not pushed down inside the pipe and not glued in. An air gap of at least two pipe diameters above the flood rim keeps waste water from ever siphoning back into the softener. The International Plumbing Code requires this kind of indirect, air-gapped waste connection for water treatment drains. Local codes vary, so confirm the exact gap and discharge point with your local plumbing authority before you finish.

**5. Connect the brine overflow line.**
Attach the overflow elbow to the side of the brine tank at the marked height, then run a separate overflow line from it to the same drain area. This line is a backup that only carries water if the brine tank ever overfills. Route it downhill the whole way, with no loops that could trap water. Do not tie it into the control head drain line.

**6. Pressurize slowly and leak-check.**
Put the bypass in the bypass position so water skips the softener for now. Open the main shutoff a quarter turn and let the house lines refill gradually. Close the open faucets once water runs steady. Walk every new joint and look for drips. Fix any leak before going further. It is far easier to chase a leak now than after the resin is wet and the tank is full.

**7. Add water and salt to the brine tank.**
Pour about three to four gallons of clean water into the brine tank. This gives the system brine to draw on the first cycle. Then add softener salt, pellets or crystals per the manufacturer, to about two-thirds full. Do not fill it to the brim on the first day. A too-full tank on a fresh install makes salt bridges more likely later. For the salt bridge problem and routine upkeep, see [how to clean a water softener](/treatment/how-to-clean-water-softener/).

**8. Program the controller and run the first regeneration.**
Set the current time, then enter your tested water hardness in grains per gallon and your iron level if you have one. These two numbers tell the head how much salt to use and how often to regenerate. Now move the bypass slowly from bypass to service. Slowly matters: easing the valve over fills the resin tank without a pressure slam that can channel or unsettle the bed. Once the tank is in service, start a manual regeneration. The head will backwash the resin, draw brine, rinse, and refill the brine tank. A full cycle runs about 90 to 120 minutes.

When the cycle finishes, run a couple of cold taps for a minute or two to clear fine resin dust and air. Your water is now soft.

## What Can Go Wrong

**Drips at the bypass or adapter joints.** Almost always an O-ring that is pinched or missing, or a plastic fitting tightened too hard and cracked. Depressurize, take the joint apart, check the O-ring seats clean, and reassemble. Plastic bypass connections are hand-tight plus a small wrench nudge, not muscle.

**Drain line backing up or not flowing.** The drain line is kinked, run uphill, or jammed into the waste pipe without an air gap. Reroute it so it falls toward the drain and discharges through a clear air gap. A drain line under back pressure can stall a regeneration mid-cycle.

**Salt level looks fine but water tests hard.** Often a salt bridge, a hard crust of salt that domes over a hollow space so the salt never reaches the water to make brine. Break it up with a broom handle. On a brand-new install, a hard test can also mean the bypass is still partly in bypass, or the hardness was entered wrong in the controller.

**Air spitting from faucets after startup.** Trapped air from filling the resin tank. Run the cold taps until it clears. If it keeps coming back, you likely have a small inlet leak pulling air on the suction side. Recheck the inlet joints.

**Slippery feel to the water.** This is normal and expected. Softened water lacks the calcium and magnesium that make hard water feel like it rinses off fast. It is not a soap residue, it is what soft water feels like.

## When to Call a Plumber

Three situations push this past a typical DIY job: a galvanized steel main that needs cutting and re-threading, no drain within reasonable reach of the install spot, and any local code that requires a permit or a licensed connection for water treatment equipment. Soldering a copper main is doable for a confident DIYer but is the step most likely to send a first-timer to a pro.

Everything else, the bypass, the adapters, the drain routing, and the startup, is within range for anyone comfortable making a watertight joint. Go slow on the leak check and slower on moving the bypass into service.
