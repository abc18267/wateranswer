Reverse osmosis is the most reliable point-of-use treatment for PFAS, lead, nitrates, arsenic, and chromium-6. No other common consumer filter technology matches it on contaminant removal range.

The catch: not all RO systems are certified for the same things. We looked at certification data, removal test results, and real-world installation experience across seven systems.

## What to Look For

**NSF/ANSI 58 certification**, the baseline. This confirms the system was independently tested for contaminant reduction.

**NSF P473**, additional certification specifically for PFOA and PFOS reduction. Not all NSF 58-certified systems carry P473.

**Lead removal data**, look for systems certified to remove lead to below 10 ppb. The best systems achieve 97, 99% reduction.

**Production capacity**, measured in gallons per day (GPD). A household of four needs at least 50 GPD. Tankless systems with 400, 600 GPD capacity essentially eliminate wait time for filtered water.

**Waste water ratio**, expressed as filtered:drain. A 1:1 ratio means you waste one gallon for every gallon filtered. 3:1 means three wasted per one filtered. Better ratio = lower water bill, less waste.

## The Certification Question

If you're buying primarily because of PFAS concerns, NSF P473 matters. Only systems that have gone through NSF's P473 testing for PFOA and PFOS can make the NSF-certified PFAS removal claim.

Waterdrop and iSpring publish their own internal PFAS test data, which shows high removal rates, but internal testing isn't the same as independent certification.

For most households, APEC's ROES-50 with NSF P473 is the cleaner choice if PFAS is the primary driver.

## Ongoing Costs

All RO systems require filter changes. Budget $50, $120/year for filter replacements depending on brand and water quality. Systems with heavy sediment loads will need pre-filter changes more often.

The RO membrane itself lasts 2, 5 years. Replacement membranes run $30, $80 depending on brand.

Track total cost of ownership over 5 years, not just purchase price. The cheapest unit may cost more in filters if it uses proprietary cartridges.
