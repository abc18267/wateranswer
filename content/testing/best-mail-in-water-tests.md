Mail-in lab tests are the only way to get accurate, quantitative results for PFAS, arsenic, VOCs, and lead at low concentrations. At-home strips are a screening step. A certified lab gives you numbers you can actually act on.

We evaluated six labs on price, test panel breadth, certification, turnaround, and report readability.

## What to Look for in a Lab

**NELAP certification** (or state equivalent). The National Environmental Laboratory Accreditation Program is the standard. A state-certified lab for your state is equivalent.

**EPA-approved methods.** For PFAS, look specifically for Method 533 or 537.1. These are the most comprehensive PFAS methods currently available. Older methods (Method 537) miss several compounds.

**Clear report format.** Lab reports vary from single-page results tables to comprehensive PDFs with historical comparisons and plain-language explanations. The best reports show your result, the MCL, and the MCLG side by side.

## What Your Results Mean

Lab reports show each contaminant's measured concentration alongside the EPA's MCL and MCLG.

"ND" (not detected) means the concentration was below the lab's reporting limit. That's not the same as zero, it means the value is too low for that method to quantify.

Any result above the MCL is an action flag. Results between the MCLG and MCL (like lead between 1 ppb and 15 ppb) are legally compliant but may warrant a filter, especially for households with children.
